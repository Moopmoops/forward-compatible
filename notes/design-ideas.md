# Forward Compatible — Design Ideas

## Framing Proposals from 2026-04-11 Discussion

### 1. Replace "religion replacement" framing with "shared worldview" section

**Current problem:** "FC as replacement for religion" is structurally accurate but emotionally oppositional. People hear "your beliefs are wrong" instead of "here's something we all need."

**Proposed reframe — "What Shared Worldviews Do":**
Position FC as describing the *shape* of a universal human need — the need for shared frameworks that provide orientation, meaning, and priorities. Religions fill this need. So do constitutions, scientific paradigms, and cultural traditions. FC doesn't compete with any of these — it describes the pattern they share when they work well, and offers a framework for building new shared understanding across difference.

Key copy direction: "FC isn't an alternative to your existing worldview. It's a lens for understanding what makes worldviews *work* — and how they can work for more people."

### 2. New concept: FC and the context problem

**The idea:** No individual can hold all the context needed for complex decisions. This is FC's deepest motivation. Forward-compatible systems aren't just nice to have — they're necessary because the future always contains information we don't have yet.

**Site treatment:** This could be a foundational section early in the page, before the principles. It establishes *why* FC matters: not because openness is virtuous in the abstract, but because closure is always premature given what we can't yet see.

### 3. New concept: Shared frameworks as distributed cognition

**The idea:** If individuals can't hold enough context alone, communities that share frameworks for integrating perspectives collectively see more than any member. FC's "include the excluded" pattern is the mechanism that makes this work — it's what keeps distributed cognition from collapsing into groupthink.

**Site treatment:** Could pair with the "orientation framework" role FC already plays. Adds a cognitive/practical dimension to what might otherwise sound purely moral.

### 4. Worldview transition as integration (composting metaphor)

**The idea:** People don't need to "abandon" old frameworks. FC models worldview change as *integration* — discovering your previous understanding was a special case of something larger. The cultivation vocabulary already supports this (composting, nutrient cycling, renewal).

**Site treatment:** This directly addresses the defensiveness problem. If people feel FC is asking them to throw away what they believe, they'll resist. If FC shows them how their existing beliefs get *included* in a larger frame, the path is open.

**Draft copy direction:** "Every worldview that ever worked got something right. Forward compatibility doesn't ask you to discard what works — it asks what else might be true alongside it."

### 5. The FC framework stack (moral / aspirational / prioritization)

**The idea:** FC wants to help build:
- A shared moral framework (what we owe each other)
- A shared aspirational framework (what we're reaching toward)  
- A shared value/prioritization framework (how we weigh competing goods)

All unified by: the commitment to include what's been excluded.

**Site treatment:** This could be a standalone section — "What FC Is Building Toward." Frames FC as ambitious but process-oriented. The aspiration toward universality *is* the method. FC doesn't claim to have universal priorities — it claims the process of seeking them, through radical inclusion, is itself forward-compatible.

### 6. Term proposal: "aspiration" as load-bearing word

Throughout the frameworks above, "aspiration" does critical work. It signals ambition without claiming achievement. "Universal priorities as aspiration" means: we don't have them yet, we may never fully have them, but the reaching-toward is the thing. This fits FC's spectrum-not-binary principle — everything is more or less forward-compatible, including our shared frameworks.

### 7. The vocabulary shift as paradigm evidence (refined)

**The idea:** FC's ecological vocabulary (cultivate, mend, tend, foster, nurture) tracks a real shift in what technology does. But the framing must avoid flattening the past — ecological knowledge and practice were always present (brewing, farming, midwifery, fermentation). What happened was that institutional/cultural framing *privileged* the mechanical and marginalized the ecological. The reintegration of ecological vocabulary is itself an FC pattern in action.

Two layers to the argument: (a) ecological vocabulary is more technically precise for 21st-century technologies that work with living systems, and (b) the mechanical vocabulary didn't just lack precision — it closed off entire categories of responsibility. "Build, deploy, optimize" has no conceptual slot for "tend the living system this technology enters."

**Site treatment:** Strengthen the vocabulary section with this dual argument. Not just "we prefer these words" but "these words are more precise AND they open categories of responsibility that the old vocabulary couldn't express."

**Draft copy direction:** "Ecological knowledge was always present alongside mechanical engineering — in farms, fermentation, medicine, soil. What changed isn't that we started doing ecological work. It's that we're finally giving it the institutional recognition and vocabulary it deserves. When technology means working with living systems, the language of tending and cultivation isn't poetic. It's precise."

**Anti-pattern to avoid:** Don't frame this as "the mechanical era gave way to the ecological era." That's a clean narrative that flattens real history. Frame it as: parallel knowledge traditions, one institutionally privileged, the other marginalized, now being reintegrated.

### 8. Indigenous polyculture as anchor example

**The idea:** Indigenous polyculture agriculture (Three Sisters, forest gardening, landscape-scale ecosystem management) is a powerful FC example because it demonstrates the pattern across multiple dimensions simultaneously: monoculture vs. polyculture as the FC spectrum; "including what monoculture excludes" producing more resilient, more productive systems; Indigenous knowledge itself as excluded knowledge now being reintegrated into Western frameworks (FC's own meta-pattern, demonstrated historically).

**Site treatment:** Could be a featured example in the examples collection (under biology/agriculture or culture/institutions). Also works as an illustration of the cultivation-vs-construction metaphor — these weren't "built" systems, they were tended relationships between species, soil, water, and people across generations.

**Sensitivity note:** Must be handled with respect and specificity — naming specific peoples and practices rather than generic "Indigenous wisdom." Avoid romanticizing or flattening diverse traditions into a single narrative. The point isn't "Indigenous people had it right and we had it wrong" — it's that excluding this knowledge made our systems less forward-compatible, and reintegrating it makes them better.

### 9. New design principle: Stewardship guidance as deliverable

**The idea:** Technologies almost never ship with guidance about the ecology they enter. Antibiotics without microbiome guidance. Social platforms without guidance on attention/trust/community effects. FC can articulate a concrete design principle: a forward-compatible technology includes stewardship guidance as a core deliverable, describing the system it enters and how to tend that system's health while using the technology.

**Site treatment:** This is one of the most actionable things FC could say. It turns philosophical principles into a design checklist. Could be part of the "design guidance" role FC already claims to play, or a standalone principle.

**Draft copy direction:** "A technology isn't finished when it works. It's finished when you can describe the living system it enters — and how to tend that system's health while using it."

### 10. Meta-principle: Don't flatten the past

**The idea:** FC should model the honest complexity it advocates for. Clean before/after narratives are cognitively tempting but they flatten history. When FC talks about its own origins (extending engineering concepts) or the traditions it draws from (ecological, Indigenous, relational), it should acknowledge parallel knowledge systems, institutional privilege, marginalization, and reintegration — not "we used to X, now we Y."

**Site treatment:** This is more of a writing guideline than a section, but it could be explicitly stated somewhere — FC practices what it preaches about inclusion by not flattening the stories of excluded knowledge traditions into tidy origin myths.
