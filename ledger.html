The Ledger

A civilizational accountability record: what states do — past, present, structural — drawing on multiple independent sources, with disagreement shown rather than hidden, and the same lens applied to every government on earth.

Premise

No single place tracks what states actually do. The pieces exist; the integration doesn't.

Human Rights Watch covers a country at a time. ACLED tracks individual events. V-Dem measures institutions across two centuries. Citizen Lab confirms spyware. EJAtlas catalogs environmental harm. Each project is excellent inside its scope. None of them, by themselves, give a citizen a complete view of how the systems of power around them are being used.

The Ledger combines them. Same record format, same verification standards, same lens for every state — including the ones whose conduct is typically softened or omitted because of geopolitical alignment.

We start with the visualization layer — the shape of what an integrated record makes possible. The ingestion pipeline is being designed alongside; live data flows incrementally as each source connector lands.

Disagreement between sources is not a bug to hide. It is the most honest finding the record can produce.

Commitments

What this record is and isn't

A few promises that have to be load-bearing from the first day or the project never earns trust.

Same lens for every state

If we document Russian conduct in Ukraine and Chinese conduct in Xinjiang, we apply the identical methodology to US conduct in detention, immigration enforcement, and overseas military operations; to European border deaths; to Saudi executions; to Israeli conduct in occupied territories. No country is exempt by virtue of geopolitical alignment.

Provenance in every record

Every claim links to the underlying source, with bias disclosed when known. We do not paraphrase past the source's confidence interval. We do not silently average estimates.

Working with, not against

HRMI, ACLED, V-Dem, OHCHR, HRW, Amnesty, SIPRI, Citizen Lab, EJAtlas — these are the projects whose work this record stands on. Our job is connection and presentation, not replacement.

Public corrections log

When a record changes — a number revised, a record removed, a tier reclassified — the change is recorded with timestamp and reason in a public log. Errors are inevitable; opacity about them isn't.

Open methodology, open data

Every ingestion script, every normalization rule, every dispute scoring weight is in the open. The data the public sees is the data the public can audit.

No government funding

Credibility on state conduct requires distance from states. The funding model has to be foundation, academic, and reader-supported — and the governance has to publish the funding sources.

Architecture

How the auto-pull-and-verify pipeline works

Four stages. Each is independent and replaceable. Records keep their full provenance chain end to end — at no point does a normalized record lose its connection to the underlying source document.

Source connectors

One connector per source. REST API where available (ACLED, UCDP, OHCHR, GDELT); CSV download on a schedule for annual datasets (V-Dem, HRMI, SIPRI); structured scrape plus LLM extraction for narrative-format sources (HRW, Amnesty, State Dept, USHMM). Each connector writes raw records to a per-source staging table.

Schema mapping

Per-source mapping rules translate raw records into the normalized schema (see methodology). Country names → ISO codes. Actor strings → canonical entity ids via embedding-based candidate matching plus LLM verification on edge cases. Original record kept alongside normalized form.

Incident triangulation

Records that describe the same incident are linked, not merged. Candidate matching by location + date + actor + summary embedding. LLM verification pass. Output is a canonical incident with the list of source records, casualty range across sources, and a confidence score derived from source count, agreement, and corroboration window.

Tier + render

Verification tier computed: multi, single, disputed, adjudicated. Records flow into the public ledger with full provenance and any disagreement preserved. Every change writes a row in the corrections log, which is public and queryable.

Build plan

What ships next

Order matters. Verification work has to land before scale work, or the ledger becomes another aggregator. The first four sources are the structural backbone — once they're flowing, everything else is incremental.

  1. ACLED + UCDP connectors
    Two event-level sources with clean APIs. Standing them up together lets cross-source agreement scoring run from day one rather than being retrofitted later.
  2. V-Dem + HRMI annual ingest
    Country-year backbone for the rights heatmap and the movers analysis. Quarterly cron. Stable, well-documented, low-maintenance.
  3. Entity resolution
    Before scaling sources, the entity-resolution layer for actor names and locations has to be solid. Mistakes here propagate everywhere. Embedding-based candidate matching plus Claude verification on edge cases.
  4. HRW + Amnesty narrative extraction
    Structured scrape of country pages and urgent actions; LLM extraction pulls (country, date, event_type, actor, casualties) into the normalized schema. Reviewer-in-the-loop for the first few hundred extractions to calibrate the prompt.
  5. Disagreement scoring
    When multiple sources cover the same incident, compute a disagreement score. Above a threshold, tier becomes 'disputed' and all source figures surface together in the public record.
  6. Structural-harms connectors
    SIPRI arms transfers, Citizen Lab spyware deployments, EJAtlas environmental cases, OFAC sanctions register. These are the categories most accountability records omit; they get equal weight here.
  7. Public corrections log
    Every change to a published record — number revised, tier reclassified, source added — writes a row to a queryable public log. The page links to this from every record.
  8. Independent governance
    An advisory board drawn from existing rights organizations, methodologists, and journalists. Publishes the funding sources. Reviews the dispute scoring weights and the source bias disclosures. This is the cultural design that lets the record outlive any one author.
